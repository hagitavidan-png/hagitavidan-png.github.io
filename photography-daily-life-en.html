Lesson opening
📔 What makes photography a source for art?

Photography is not only documentation. It is a way to choose, frame, emphasize, and tell a story. In this lesson, a photograph becomes the starting point for a personal artwork.

👁️
See again
Discover beauty, meaning, and emotion in moments that may seem completely ordinary at first.
✂️
Choose crop and focus
Notice how angle, distance, light, and shadow change the image and the story it tells.
🎨
Translate photo into drawing
Use the photograph as a starting point for a quiet, poetic, dramatic, or personal interpretation rather than a direct copy.
💭
Find a small story
A cup on a table, an open window, afternoon light, or an almost empty street can all become the subject of an artwork.
"Even a small moment from the day can become a precise, quiet image with presence."

Artists and references
🔗 Inspiration sources
Ed Ruscha
Tate · Everyday Life
Everyday imagery in a precise visual language.
Ruscha works with gas stations, signs, facades, and urban landscapes. In his art, everyday imagery appears with directness and precision and becomes part of a highly recognizable visual language.
Read more ↗
David Hockney
Tate · Time and Looking
Several angles and moments within a single image.
Hockney creates paintings and photographic collages in which one scene is built from multiple viewpoints, angles, and moments. In his joiners, many separate photographs are connected into one lively, fragmented image full of movement.
Read more ↗
Herbert Bayer
Tate · Experimental Looking
Photography as an experiment in viewpoint, crop, and form.
Bayer created photographs and visual experiments that combine unusual angles, sharp crops, and distorted viewpoints. In his work, photography becomes an experimental tool that disrupts conventional ways of seeing body, space, and image.

Observation tools
🪟 How to approach artistic photography?

Before you begin to draw, pause with the photograph and ask what truly attracts you in it.

💡
Light
Is the light soft or sharp? Does it create drama, calm, warmth, or a feeling of memory?
🧭
Focus
What does the eye meet first? What matters most for the viewer to feel when looking?
🪄
Omission
You do not have to draw everything. Sometimes omission creates a stronger and more precise work.
🌈
Interpretation
You can change color, soften the background, heighten contrast, or build a different atmosphere from the original photograph.
Practice
🖌️ Practical exercise

Choose one photograph from your life — a table, a window, a plant, a cup, a street, a corner of the house, or a small moment from the day — and turn it into the starting point for an artwork.

1
Choose a photo
Choose a simple image that contains something that draws you in: light, story, stillness, composition, or color.
2
Observation
Ask yourself what is truly essential in the photo — and what can be left out.
3
First sketch
Make a quick sketch of the overall structure, the composition, and the relationships between the shapes.
4
Personal interpretation
Change one small thing — color, crop, background, focus, or mood — so the work truly becomes yours.
Tip: the goal is not to copy the photograph, but to listen to what it suggests and build a personal visual language from it.
